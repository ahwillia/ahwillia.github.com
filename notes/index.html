Notes and Resources

I want to use this page as a resource for undergrads who are interested in computational neuroscience. Below are some sources of information that you may find interesting/helpful.

#1 | Papers

It is obviously important to read primary research papers that are specific to the topics you are interested in. Below are some papers that give an overview of the field of computational neuroscience, as well as it's history, and culture.

  • Theoretical Neuroscience Rising
    • LF Abbott (2008). Neuron. 60(3):489-95.
    "Theoretical neuroscience is sometimes criticized for not making enough predictions... To apply prediction as the ultimate test of a theory is a distortion of history. Many of the most celebrated moments in quantitative science — the gravitational basis of the shape of planetary orbits, the quantum basis of the spectrum of the hydrogen atom, and the relativistic origin of the precession of the orbit of Mercury — involved postdictions of known and well-characterized phenomena."

  • Theory and Simulation in Neuroscience
    • W Gerstner, H Sprekeler, G Deco (2012). Science. 338(6103):60-5
    "Modern neuroscience methods requiring extensive training have led to a specialization of researchers, such that neuroscience today is fragmented into labs working on genes and molecules, on single-cell electrophysiology, on multineuron recordings, and on cognitive neuroscience and psychophysics, to name just a few. One of the central tasks of computational neuroscience is to bridge these different levels of description by simulation and mathematical theory."

  • Why Are Computational Neuroscience and Systems Biology So Separate?
    • E De Schutter (2008). PLoS Comput Biol. 4(5): e1000078.
    "Ideally one would want to promote stronger interaction between the two fields and increased awareness of each other's strengths, i.e., by organizing joint meetings. But at present it seems there is... little interest in the respective communities for such initiatives. In part, this reflects different interests, e.g., system biologists often see computational neuroscience work as too specialized, while many computational neuroscientists have little interests in genes and molecules."

#3 | Online Courses and Videos
  • Computational Neuroscience
    • Especially good lectures on Bayesian inference and linear-nonlinear models. Some mathematics background will be useful.

  • Neural Networks and Biological Modeling
    • This very nice set of lectures by Wulfram Gerstner complements the above course by Fairhall and Rao. The first part of the course on single neuron models is particularly essential and well-introduced. Prof. Gerstner is also offering a similar course on edX.

  • Computational Methods for Data Analysis
    • This course is excellent. As the title suggests, the course is not limited to the field of neuroscience, but all of the material is incredibly important and relevant: Time-Frequency analysis, principal components analysis (and SVD), independent component analysis (ICA), and more.

#4 | Books
  • Dynamical Systems in Neuroscience

    • This book is an excellent starting point for those who are interested in learning some basic mathematical analysis of dynamical systems. Izhikevich focuses on single-compartment conductance-based models of individual neurons, providing an excellent foundation for understanding all neural systems. (Prerequisites: some linear algebra, experience with ODEs).


  • An Introduction to Systems Biology

    • While this book mentions neuronal networks, the primary focus is on the dynamics of transcriptional networks in E. coli. But this is nevertheless one of my favorite books. Uri Alon outlines many basic theoretical principles to describe the E. coli system, and describes carefully designed experiments that validate these models. Alon's highly successful approach to systems biology should be more widely appreciated in the neuroscience community. Also see Uri Alon's online lectures. (Prerequisites: None)

  • Theoretical Neuroscience

    • This is a classic graduate-level textbook that covers both analytical and computational modeling techniques in a diverse number of neural systems. If you are serious about computational neuroscience, you will need to read this book at some point. But be warned, it can be dense. (Prerequisites: A class in neurophysiology, linear algebra, and differential equations).


  • Applied Linear Regression Models

    • If you want to do any work with experimental data, you need to learn statistics on a deep mathematical level. This book was required reading for one of my undergraduate statistics courses, and I found it particularly helpful. There are undoubtedly other books you could use to this end, but this is the text I have experience with and it is didactically sound. You can also look into Biostatistical Analysis by Jerold Zar - it is a great textbook for reference, but a dry read. (Prerequisites: a class in probability/statistics, linear algebra, experience with R).
