Current Projects
#1 | Exploration of homeostatic conductance regulation rules in single-compartment model neurons

Neuronal function is largely determined by the expression of ion channel proteins. There are a large number of ion channel proteins, each with distinct biophysical properties. The problem for a neuron is this: ion channels are produced and degraded in a stochastic and noisy manner, but the neuron must maintain appropriate electrical properties for the brain to function properly. Eve has given a great analogy for this:

"[Ion channels] last only for weeks, days or sometimes hours, so every single neuron has to constantly replace every single protein. So if you look at a 98-year-old, he or she replaced every single kind of ion channel thousands and thousands of times. That's equivalent to, for example, taking a 777 airplane and replacing every single component while it's flying, without showing disruption of function!"

Neurons achieve this amazing feat by homeostatically tuning their expression of ion channels, which has given rise to the field of neuronal homeostasis.

A number of previous studies have developed computational models of homeostatic channel regulation. I am working with Tim O'Leary in the Marder Lab to examine the common principles of these different models, and develop new biologically plausible models. One of our findings is that many different homeostatic rules can be designed to produce similar neuronal function. Additionally, one may be able to determine the form of these homeostatic rules in biological data by examining the correlations between different ion channel types at different stages of development.

#2 | Examination of the crustacean cardiac neuromuscular transform

While much is known about vertebrate motor systems, invertebrate model organisms present the best opportunity to dissect the functions of individual neurons, neuromodulators, and muscle groups. More specifically, simple invertebrate systems such as the Aplysia feeding network, the crustacean stomatogastric ganglion, and the crustacean heart have been mapped in exquisite detail by decades of research (thanks to their relative anatomical simplicity). We know, for example, that the heart of the American lobster (H. americanus) is rhythmically stimulated by exactly 5 motor neurons in every preparation, and that these motor neurons are solely responsible for heart contractions. We can therefore ask a specialized set of questions in this system:

  • How does the neural activity of these 5 cells translate into muscle activity?
  • How direct or straightforward is this transformation?
  • What other factors (e.g. temperature, or biological signals) influence the neuromuscular interface?
  • How variable is neural activity across preparations? To what extent does this correlate with variability in muscle activity?

    • Some of these questions have been previously studied in a theoretical context (for example, Brezina et al. 2000a, 2000b, 2000c). I am examining the extent to which these current theories capture experimental reality by simultaneously measuring baseline neural activity and muscle behavior across many different preparations.
